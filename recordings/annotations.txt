Script:
  "In distributed systems, a quorum is when a majority of nodes — more than half — must
  agree before a decision is made. The key property of quorums is the overlap guarantee:
  any two quorums must share at least one node in common, which is what ensures
  consistency across the system. Quorums are commonly used for leader election, where
  nodes vote to decide which one should act as the primary. Without this, you can run
  into the split-brain problem, where two nodes both believe they are the leader at the
  same time, which can lead to conflicting writes and data corruption.

  To prevent problems that arise from split-brain, distributed systems use fencing tokens.
  A fencing token is a monotonically increasing number that gets issued every time a lock
  or lease is granted. The important part is that resources must validate the token before
  accepting any operation — if a request comes in with an older token than what was last
  seen, the resource rejects it. This ensures that even if an old leader wakes up and tries
  to write, the storage system will refuse its requests because it's carrying a stale token."

  "There are two main categories of faults in distributed systems. A crash fault is the
  simpler one — a node either works correctly or it stops responding entirely. It fails
  by stopping. A Byzantine fault is much more severe: a node can behave arbitrarily,
  sending conflicting or incorrect messages, or even acting maliciously. It might lie
  to some nodes but not others.

  Byzantine fault tolerance is required in environments like blockchain networks,
  aerospace systems, and multi-party computation where participants may have competing
  interests. Most datacenters assume crash faults.

  Byzantine fault tolerance is expensive [Too vague]. Trust is important in choosing a protocol

  honest dramatically simplifies the problem. When you only need to tolerate stops, your

  tolerant systems are much more common in practice.

  datacenter the extra cost of BFT isn't
  justified. In an open system like a blockchain you

Script:
  "Distributed systems are designed around different assumptions about timing. In the
  synchronous model, all messages are guaranteed to arrive within a known bounded time,
  and processes respond within a known deadline. At the other extreme, the asynchronous
  model makes no timing assumptions at all — messages can be delayed arbitrarily and
  there are no reliable clocks. In between is the partially synchronous model, where
  the system behaves synchronously most of the time but can occasionally exceed those
  bounds. That's actually the most realistic model for real-world systems like cloud
  infrastructure, where networks are usually fast but can have unpredictable delays.

  Safety and liveness are two fundamental properties we care about in distributed
  algorithms. A safety property says that something bad never happens — if it's ever
  violated, you can point to a specific moment in time when the violation occurred.
  Examples include uniqueness — no two nodes hold the same lock at the same time — or
  consistency, meaning you never get conflicting reads. A liveness property says that
  something good eventually happens — the system keeps making progress. Examples are
  availability, meaning every request eventually gets a response, or termination, meaning
  an algorithm eventually reaches a decision.

  The tension between safety and liveness is a central challenge in distributed systems.
  Under a network partition, it's often impossible to guarantee both at the same time,
  which is essentially what the CAP theorem is about.

  When it comes to failure models, in the crash-recovery model a node that crashes loses
  all of its state permanently — both in-memory and on disk — so it can never rejoin the
  cluster. A fail-stop node, on the other hand, is one that crashes but can restart and
  rejoin after recovery. A fail-recovery node is one that stops permanently and never
  comes back. System models are primarily useful for guiding implementation decisions
  and choosing the right data structures, rather than for formally proving correctness."
